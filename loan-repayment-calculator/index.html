How this loan repayment calculator works

This loan repayment calculator estimates your monthly payment, total interest, and payoff timeline for personal loans, car loans, and other fixed‑rate installments. Enter the loan amount, APR, and term, then optionally add extra monthly payments. The calculator shows how extra payments reduce interest and can bring your payoff date forward. If your loan uses fees, variable rates, or penalties for early repayment, treat the result as an estimate and check your agreement. For planning, compare two scenarios: the minimum payment and a small extra payment (even 1–5% of the payment can make a meaningful difference).

Practical tips: try paying a small fixed extra amount each month; compare a 3‑year vs 5‑year term to see total interest; avoid stretching the term just to lower the payment if total cost becomes high. Limitations: results assume fixed rates and steady payments/contributions. If your situation changes (rate changes, fees, irregular income), rerun the calculator with updated inputs to keep your plan accurate.

How loan payments work

With most fixed-rate loans, each monthly payment includes interest (the cost of borrowing) and principal (paying down the amount you owe). Early payments usually include more interest; later payments include more principal.

Extra payments

Any extra payment you add is applied to your principal. That reduces future interest and often shortens the payoff time.

FAQ

Does a lower interest rate always reduce the monthly payment?

Yes — for the same loan amount and term, a lower APR reduces monthly interest and lowers the required payment.

What if APR is 0%?

If APR is 0%, your payment is simply the loan amount divided by the number of months.

Why might my real lender payment differ?

Lenders may use different compounding conventions, add fees, or require escrow/insurance (especially for mortgages). This tool is a clean estimate for planning.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does a loan repayment calculator work?

It estimates your monthly payment and total interest using the loan amount, interest rate, and term, based on standard amortization math.

What is amortization?

Amortization means each payment covers interest plus some principal. Early payments are mostly interest; later payments pay down more principal.

Can I see an amortization schedule?

Yes. A schedule shows how each payment is split between interest and principal over time so you can track progress.

What happens if I make extra payments?

Extra payments usually reduce total interest and shorten the loan term because they reduce the remaining principal faster.

Is the interest rate fixed?

This calculator assumes a fixed rate. Variable rates can change payments and total cost.

Calculator methodology & assumptions

This calculator uses standard financial formulas and simplified assumptions (for example: constant rates, regular payments, and rounding). Real-world results can differ due to fees, taxes, rate changes, compounding conventions, and account rules.
